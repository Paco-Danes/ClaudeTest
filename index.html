Explanation

The answer is actually richer than a single cell type — here's what the research shows:

The Four Developmental Cellular States (Neftel et al., 2019, Cell)

The landmark single-cell RNA-seq study by Neftel et al. showed that malignant cells in glioblastoma exist in four cellular states that recapitulate normal developmental cell types: neural progenitor-like (NPC-like), oligodendrocyte progenitor-like (OPC-like), astrocyte-like (AC-like), and mesenchymal-like (MES-like). Critically, these meta-modules mimic developmental cell types but with important distortions from normal programs. The AC-like state was most highly expressed by astrocytes but was also expressed in radial glia (RG) and contained RG markers such as HOPX.

Each state is driven by specific genetic alterations: CDK4 amplification favors the NPC-like state, PDGFRA amplification favors OPC-like, EGFR amplification favors AC-like, and NF1 loss favors MES-like.

oRG-like Cells (Bhaduri et al., 2020, Cell Stem Cell)

As you noted, Bhaduri et al. identified an invasive glioma stem cell population similar to outer radial glia (oRG), a fetal cell type that expands the stem cell niche in normal human cortex. These oRG-like cells undergo mitotic somal translocation (MST), a behavior normally only seen during development, which promotes glioblastoma invasiveness. Because MST doesn't normally occur outside development, targeting it may be a viable therapeutic strategy. The surface marker PTPRZ1 marks these cells.

Glial Progenitor Cells as the Hierarchy Hub (Couturier et al., 2020, Nature Communications)

Couturier et al. performed scRNA-seq on over 53,000 adult GBM cells and 22,000 normal fetal brain cells and found a conserved neural tri-lineage cancer hierarchy centered around glial progenitor-like cells. This progenitor population contains the majority of the cancer's cycling cells and, using RNA velocity, is often the originator of the other cell types.

Therapeutic Implications

Given that you're designing a therapy, here are the key takeaways from this developmental framing:

The high plasticity between states is a major challenge — a single GBM cell can generate all four states, and GBM cells exist in gradients of transcriptomic states rather than fixed identities. This means targeting a single state may allow escape through state transitions.

Some promising angles include targeting PTPRZ1/MST to block oRG-mediated invasion, targeting the developmental signaling pathways that maintain the progenitor-like state (NOTCH, SHH, WNT), and exploiting the genetic driver–state associations (e.g., EGFR inhibition in AC-dominant tumors). Recent multi-omics work has also shown that AP-1 transcription factor activity is high in tumor core cells while BACH1 is higher at the infiltrative margin, and combined inhibition of AP-1 and BACH1 was more effective than either alone in mouse models.

So the developmental cell types you're likely remembering are neural progenitor cells (NPCs) and oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs) — along with oRG, these are the fetal/developmental populations whose programs GBM hijacks. Would you like me to dig deeper into any specific therapeutic angle?

2. Canopy cells — NDNF+/NPY− Canopy cells sit closer to the pia surface and have elaborate axons that can send collaterals into deeper layers. They are NDNF-positive like NGFCs but lack NPY and are electrophysiologically distinct — they are non-late-spiking and accommodating rather than exhibiting the classic late-spiking phenotype. The Gouwens et al. Why the hypothalamus stands out for neurotransmitter diversity:

The hypothalamus contains the highest diversity of neurons in the brain, with many of these neurons capable of co-releasing neurotransmitters and neuropeptides in a use-dependent manner. This was established by single-cell RNA sequencing that distinguished 62 neuronal subtypes producing glutamatergic, dopaminergic, or GABAergic markers for synaptic neurotransmission, with the ability to engage in task-dependent neurotransmitter switching.

The 2023 whole-brain atlas studies (Allen Brain Cell Atlas and companion Slide-seq work) reinforced this, finding that cell type diversity was remarkably high in the midbrain, hindbrain, and hypothalamus, with most clusters requiring at least three discrete gene expression markers to uniquely define them. Critically, neuropeptide signaling has been particularly strongly associated with the hypothalamus, where many neuropeptides were originally biochemically discovered.

What makes it so neurochemically rich:

The hypothalamus hosts neurons using virtually every major classical neurotransmitter system — glutamate, GABA, and dopamine as fast/modulatory transmitters — plus it is the exclusive home of histaminergic neurons. Histaminergic neurons are found exclusively in the tuberomammillary nucleus of the hypothalamus, and all co-release GABA. On top of that, it expresses an extraordinary number of neuropeptides: oxytocin, vasopressin, CRH, TRH, GnRH, orexin/hypocretin, MCH, somatostatin, GHRH, NPY, AgRP, POMC/α-MSH, galanin, neurotensin, and many more. Implications for your drug off-target analysis:

This makes the hypothalamus the region where a broad-spectrum neuromodulatory drug would encounter the widest variety of neurotransmitter systems simultaneously. A drug targeting, say, monoamine receptors would not only affect dopaminergic neurons (arcuate/tuberoinfundibular) and histaminergic neurons (tuberomammillary), but could also interact with the dense neuropeptidergic signaling that modulates feeding, circadian rhythm, stress response, reproduction, and autonomic function. The sheer density of co-transmission — where single neurons release multiple signaling molecules — means that perturbing one system almost inevitably cascades into others within the hypothalamus, making off-target prediction especially challenging there.

This is a great question that maps directly onto the Allen Brain Cell Atlas whole-mouse-brain dataset from Yao et al. (2023, Nature). Let me walk through what we know.

What counts as "rare-transmitter"

The atlas classified mature neurons into eight partially overlapping groups based on neurotransmitter transporter expression: glutamatergic, GABAergic, serotonergic, dopaminergic, cholinergic, glycinergic, noradrenergic, and histaminergic. Glutamatergic and GABAergic neurons made up about 63% and 36% of total neurons respectively, while serotonergic, dopaminergic, cholinergic, glycinergic, noradrenergic, and histaminergic neurons collectively accounted for only roughly 2% of all neurons. Those six modulatory types are your "rare-transmitter" populations.

The most broadly expressed neuropeptide: Cck (cholecystokinin)

The atlas measured cell-type specificity using the Tau score and found that some neuropeptides are expressed widely across many clusters at high levels — with Cck, Pnoc, Adcyap1, Penk, Sst, and Tac1 heading the list — while others are restricted to just one or a few clusters. Of this group, Cck consistently ranked as the single most broadly expressed neuropeptide gene, appearing in the largest number of transcriptomic clusters brain-wide. Because it has the lowest cell-type specificity (lowest Tau score), it is also the neuropeptide most frequently detected across the rare-transmitter neuron classes — it is prominently co-expressed in dopaminergic, serotonergic, and cholinergic clusters among others.

Brain regions where you'd be acting on CCK-expressing neurons

CCK expression is remarkably widespread, but the key regions to anticipate include:

The cerebral cortex (especially interneuron populations throughout isocortex), the hippocampal formation (where CCK marks a prominent basket cell interneuron subtype), the amygdala, the hypothalamus (multiple nuclei), the ventral tegmental area and substantia nigra (where CCK is classically co-expressed with dopamine), the midbrain raphe nuclei (co-expressed with serotonin), and parts of the hindbrain. The companion MERFISH atlas showed that many neuropeptide genes have spatially heterogeneous distributions, with some being enriched in specific nuclei across the hypothalamus, striatum, and other regions.

Behavioral consequences to anticipate

Because CCK is distributed so broadly and cuts across so many rare-transmitter systems simultaneously, modulating it would produce a complex behavioral phenotype spanning reward and motivation (via VTA dopaminergic co-expression), mood and anxiety (via serotonergic and amygdala circuits), memory and spatial processing (via hippocampal interneurons), feeding behavior and satiety (via hypothalamic circuits), and cortical network excitability (via interneuron modulation throughout neocortex). This is why CCK receptor pharmacology has been notoriously difficult to translate — receptor subtype (CCK-A vs CCK-B), regional specificity of delivery, and which transmitter system you're intersecting all matter enormously for the behavioral readout.

If you want to narrow your pharmacological target, I'd suggest working from the receptor side (CCKAR vs CCKBR have quite different distributions) or using intersectional genetic strategies to restrict manipulation to CCK within a single rare-transmitter class. Happy to dig deeper into any of these angles.

Here's what the data show:

The hindbrain, midbrain, and hypothalamus contained substantially greater numbers of neuronal cell types than the other brain regions. This wasn't simply because these areas have more anatomical sub-nuclei. The local complexity of neuronal cell-type composition was also substantially higher in the midbrain, hindbrain, and hypothalamus, indicating that these regions were not simply composed of more subregions with distinct cell compositions, but also had complex local neighborhoods with higher cellular diversity.

An independent study using snRNA-seq paired with Slide-seq confirmed this: in total, they identified roughly 5,000 clusters of cells, mostly neuronal, with the diversity distributed primarily in subcortical areas — most especially in the midbrain, pons, medulla, and hypothalamus.

But there's a critical nuance about the quality of that heterogeneity. The Yao et al. Allen Institute atlas revealed a striking dorsal–ventral dichotomy. The dorsal part of the brain contains relatively fewer yet highly divergent neuronal types, whereas the ventral part contains more numerous neuronal types that are more closely related to each other. More specifically, in regions with larger numbers of clusters — hypothalamus, midbrain, and hindbrain — the clusters are more similar to each other, suggesting lower transcriptomic differences and less hierarchical organization. By contrast, regions like the cerebellum, thalamus, and pallium have fewer clusters but with much greater pairwise transcriptomic divergence.

What this means for your atlas effort:

If your goal is to discover the largest number of previously uncharacterized cell types, the hypothalamus is probably your highest-yield target. It has extraordinary neuronal diversity, complex neuropeptide co-expression patterns, irregularly shaped and partially overlapping spatial distributions of cell types, and historically has been under-profiled relative to cortex. Many hypothalamic cell populations require at least three discrete marker genes to uniquely identify them, meaning shallow profiling will miss them.

However, if your goal is to understand maximally distinct cell programs (e.g., for building genetic access tools or understanding fundamentally different circuit logic), the dorsal structures — particularly thalamus and cerebellum — offer fewer types that are more transcriptomically separable and hierarchically organized, which can be advantageous for certain downstream applications.

For pure discovery yield, though: hypothalamus first, then midbrain/hindbrain.
